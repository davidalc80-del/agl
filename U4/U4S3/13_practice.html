1.3. Practice

🧠 Exercise 1 – Identify the Mental Timeline

Instructions: Read each sentence and select whether it describes a past cause + past consequence (third conditional) or past cause + present consequence (mixed conditional). Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

  • 1. If we had updated the software, the system wouldn’t be crashing now.
  • 2. If I had replied to the client, we would have secured the deal yesterday.
  • 3. If they had trained the staff, we wouldn’t be short-staffed today.
  • 4. If she had submitted the proposal, it would have been approved last week.

💡 Tip: Third conditionals imagine a different past; mixed conditionals link a past mistake to a present problem.

🧠 Exercise 2 – Match the Scenario to the Mental Simulation

Instructions: Match each workplace scenario to the correct conditional sentence by selecting from the dropdown. Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

Scenario Sentence Feedback
You missed a deadline, causing stress now.
A wrong order was sent due to a past error.
The client was satisfied because you acted promptly in the past.
A project failed due to poor planning.

💡 Tip: Match the timeline in the scenario to the sentence: past consequences for third conditionals, present consequences for mixed.

🧠 Exercise 3 – Complete the Sentence

Instructions: Complete each sentence by typing the correct third or mixed conditional form in the text box. Use the verbs provided. Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

  • 1. If we ______ (inform) the team earlier, we ______ (avoid) the confusion now.
  • 2. If I ______ (review) the contract, it ______ (be) signed yesterday.
  • 3. If they ______ (schedule) the meeting properly, we ______ (not/rush) today.
  • 4. If she ______ (test) the equipment, it ______ (work) during the presentation.

💡 Tip: Use “had + past participle” for the ‘if’ clause; use “would have + past participle” for past results or “would + base verb” for present results.

Extra Practice – Web Resources for Third and Mixed Conditionals

🎯 Why Use These Resources?

To master third and mixed conditionals, practice is key. These online resources offer interactive exercises, clear explanations, and practical examples to help you use these structures confidently in workplace scenarios, such as project reviews, client apologies, or team discussions. Each website below provides activities that reinforce the grammar from Session 3, focusing on professional communication.

💡 Tip: As you use these resources, try to apply the conditionals to real workplace situations, like explaining a missed deadline or reflecting on a project mistake.

📚 Recommended Web Resources

Explore these trusted websites for additional practice with third and mixed conditionals. They offer a variety of exercises, from gap-fills to quizzes, designed for intermediate learners like you.

1. British Council – LearnEnglish

The British Council’s LearnEnglish site offers clear explanations of third and mixed conditionals, with interactive exercises like multiple-choice questions and gap-fills. The examples are practical and relevant, helping you understand how to use these structures in everyday communication.

Link: Conditionals: Third and Mixed

Why It’s Useful: The site includes workplace-relevant examples, such as “If we had moved faster, we would have met the deadline,” and interactive tasks to test your understanding.

💡 Tip: Try the grammar test after reading the explanation to check your progress.

2. Test-English

Test-English provides detailed grammar explanations and a variety of exercises, including gap-fills and multiple-choice quizzes, for mixed conditionals. The site focuses on both past-to-present and present-to-past hypothetical scenarios, which are useful for workplace reflections.

Link: Mixed Conditionals

Why It’s Useful: The exercises encourage you to think about real-life situations, like “If I had prepared better, I wouldn’t be nervous now,” which mirrors workplace challenges.

💡 Tip: Complete the three online exercises to practice different types of mixed conditionals.

3. AgendaWeb

AgendaWeb offers a collection of mixed conditional exercises, including fill-in-the-blank and sentence-writing tasks. These are great for practicing the structure and applying it to various contexts.

Link: Mixed Conditional Exercises

Why It’s Useful: The site provides multiple quizzes that test your ability to choose the correct verb forms, helping you build accuracy in professional writing or speaking.

💡 Tip: Try the “Mixed conditionals quiz” to challenge yourself with different sentence types.

4. Perfect English Grammar

Perfect English Grammar offers clear explanations and interactive exercises for third and mixed conditionals, including downloadable PDFs for offline practice. The examples are straightforward and applicable to workplace scenarios.

Link: Conditional Exercises

Why It’s Useful: The site includes exercises like “If we had trained the staff, we wouldn’t be facing issues now,” which are relevant for discussing workplace mistakes.

💡 Tip: Download the PDF quizzes for extra practice you can do anytime.

5. GrammarBank

GrammarBank provides a range of mixed conditional exercises, including fill-in-the-blank and multiple-choice tasks, with answers for self-checking. The exercises focus on practical applications, making them ideal for vocational training students.

Link: Mixed Conditional Exercise

Why It’s Useful: The site’s exercises, like “If I had attended the meeting, I would know the plan now,” help you practice conditionals in professional contexts.

💡 Tip: Use the answer key to check your work and understand any mistakes.

🧠 How to Use These Resources

To get the most out of these websites:

  • Start with the explanations to review the grammar rules.
  • Complete the interactive exercises and check your answers to build accuracy.
  • Try rewriting the example sentences using workplace scenarios, like missed deadlines or client issues, to make them relevant to your studies.
  • Use the vocabulary from Unit 4 (e.g., “deal with,” “sort out”) in your answers to connect the grammar to professional communication.

Combine these online exercises with the “Mistakes at Work” video activities to practice conditionals in real-world contexts. The more you apply these structures, the more natural they’ll become!

💡 Tip: Set a goal to complete one exercise from each site and note down any new workplace-related examples you find.
