2.1. Writing lesson

Writing Lesson – Professional Emails

📝 Why Writing Matters

Writing clear, polite, and professional emails is a key skill in the workplace, whether you’re addressing a supplier about a problem or responding to a customer’s complaint. This lesson prepares you to write effective emails that describe issues, request solutions, or offer support, using a formal and reassuring tone.

You’ll develop CEFR text production strategies, such as planning your message, organizing it logically, and expressing your purpose clearly, to ensure your emails are professional and effective.

💡 Tip: Always consider who you’re writing to (e.g., supplier, customer) to choose the right tone and level of formality.

🧠 Key Language Tools

To write effective emails, you’ll use a range of language tools from Unit 4, including conditionals, polite requests, and vocabulary for problem-solving. These tools help you explain issues, show empathy, and propose solutions.

Tool Purpose Example
Conditionals (1st, 2nd, 3rd, Mixed) Explain possibilities, past mistakes, or current impacts If we had checked the order, we would have avoided this issue. (3rd)
If you confirm today, we can ship tomorrow. (1st)
Polite Requests Ask for action respectfully Please let us know how you plan to resolve this.
Unit 4 Vocabulary Describe problems and solutions We’re working on it to sort out the delay.
Wish / If Only Express regret or desired outcomes We wish the delivery had arrived on time.

💡 Tip: Combine conditionals with Unit 4 phrases like “deal with” or “follow up” to sound natural and professional.

✍️ Writing Scaffolding

Use this structure to write clear and professional emails for the practice and gradable tasks:

  • Greeting: Use a formal opening, e.g., “Dear [Name/Team].”
  • Introduction: Briefly state the purpose, e.g., “I’m writing regarding a recent delivery issue.”
  • Describe the Problem: Explain the issue clearly, using conditionals or “wish/if only” to show what happened or what could have been different (e.g., “If the goods had been inspected, this error would have been avoided.”).
  • Explain Impact or Context: Mention the consequences, possibly using a conditional (e.g., “If the delivery had arrived on time, we wouldn’t be delayed now.”).
  • Request or Offer Solution: Politely ask for action or propose a solution, e.g., “Please let us know how you can address this” or “We’d like to offer a refund.”
  • Closing: End with a polite sign-off, e.g., “Thank you for your attention. Best regards, [Your Name].”

Example Email (B2C):
Dear Ms. Smith,
I’m writing to address your recent complaint about a delayed delivery. We’re sorry for the inconvenience; if the courier had followed the schedule, you would have received your package on time. We wish this hadn’t happened. To resolve this, we’d like to offer a replacement or a full refund. Please let us know your preference.
Best regards,
[Your Name]

💡 Tip: Keep your email concise, using short sentences and clear language to ensure professionalism.

🎯 Preparing for the Tasks

The writing tasks will involve:

  • Practice Task (B2B): Writing an email to a supplier about defective goods or a delay, requesting a solution.
  • Gradable Task (B2C): Responding to a customer complaint about a delivery issue, apologizing, and proposing a solution.

To prepare:

  • Review Unit 4 vocabulary (e.g., “sort out,” “we’re working on it”) to describe problems and solutions.
  • Practice using conditionals to explain issues or possibilities, but don’t rely on them exclusively.
  • Focus on a polite, formal tone, using phrases like “We apologize for the inconvenience” or “Please let us know.”

💡 Tip: Before writing, plan your email by noting the problem, impact, and solution to ensure a clear structure.
