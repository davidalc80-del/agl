2.2. Grammar Lesson

Grammar Focus: Modal Verbs in Workplace Ethics

What Are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs let you express attitudes like necessity, possibility, permission, or advice—key for discussing ethics at work. In this session, we’ll explore how they shape professional communication, drawing from the reading “Building an Ethical Company” and real-world workplace scenarios.

Key modal verbs: must, mustn’t, have to, don’t have to, should, ought to, can, could, might.

💡 Tip: Focus on how these verbs reflect the speaker’s perspective on ethical choices.

🔍 Understanding Modal Verbs Through Meaning

Rather than memorizing rules, think about what these verbs reveal about the speaker’s view—how urgent, possible, or permissible an action is in the context of workplace ethics.

Modal Verb Meaning Example Interpretation
must Strong necessity, often driven by duty or ethics. Managers and leaders must help their teams understand what is right and what is wrong. The speaker sees this as a non-negotiable responsibility for ethical leadership.
have to External requirement, often from rules or norms. You have to make good decisions not just when someone is watching, but all the time. The speaker highlights a workplace expectation rooted in ethical standards.
mustn’t Strong prohibition, warning against harmful actions. You mustn’t think that ethics only matters in official documents. The speaker firmly rejects a limited view of ethics, emphasizing its daily importance.
don’t have to Lack of obligation, indicating freedom from requirement. Employees don’t have to attend optional ethics workshops, but they’re encouraged to. The speaker clarifies that participation is voluntary, offering flexibility.
should Recommendation, suggesting a beneficial action. Companies should talk about values every day. The speaker advises a practice that strengthens ethical culture, with some flexibility.
ought to Formal recommendation, often tied to moral duty. A mentor ought to help younger workers think about their decisions. The speaker gently urges mentors to guide ethically, with a sense of responsibility.
can Ability or permission, enabling ethical action. Employees can learn and grow as ethical professionals in a supportive workplace. The speaker highlights the opportunity for ethical development.
could Possibility or tentative suggestion for ethical choices. We could organize team discussions to address workplace gossip. The speaker proposes a potential strategy, leaving room for consideration.
might Uncertainty or low probability, often speculative. Ignoring harassment might damage the company’s reputation. The speaker warns of a possible negative outcome, urging caution.

💡 Tip: Notice how each modal shapes the speaker’s stance on ethics—some are firm, others flexible.

🗣️ Form and Use

Modal verbs are followed by a base verb and don’t change for person or number. Here’s how they work in professional contexts:

Affirmative

  • You must report unethical behavior to HR.
  • Employees have to complete ethics training annually.
  • We should promote fairness in team decisions.
  • Mentors ought to model respectful communication.
  • Staff can suggest improvements to the code of conduct.
  • We could hold a workshop on equality next month.
  • A small oversight might lead to bigger ethical issues.

Negative

  • You mustn’t ignore complaints about harassment.
  • Interns don’t have to lead ethics discussions.
  • You shouldn’t favor certain team members over others.
  • We can’t allow gossip to undermine team trust.

Questions

  • Should we revise our ethical guidelines?
  • Do managers have to monitor team conflicts?
  • Can employees report issues anonymously?
  • Could we invite a mentor to discuss integrity?
  • Might this decision affect our company’s reputation?

💡 Tip: Practice forming sentences to express your own ethical priorities.

Compare and Reflect

Look at these sentences—one from the text, one professional example:

  • From text: Managers and leaders must help their teams understand what is right and what is wrong.
  • Professional example: Employees can suggest improvements to the code of conduct.

Question: How do these modals differ?

Must conveys a strong, essential duty, signaling that ethical guidance is critical. Can opens up possibilities, inviting employees to contribute without pressure. Each reflects a different stance on ethical responsibility.

💡 Tip: Think about how word choice shapes expectations in your workplace.

🚀 Apply It

Reflect on these questions to connect modal verbs to your professional context:

  • What’s one thing you must do to maintain integrity in your vocational field?
  • Is there something you mustn’t do to avoid harming your workplace’s ethical culture?
  • What should a colleague do if they notice unfair treatment in your industry?
  • How can you contribute to a respectful work environment in your future role?
  • How might ignoring a small ethical issue affect your team in the long run?

💡 Tip: Use these questions to practice modals in sentences about your career goals.
