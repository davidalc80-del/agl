2.3. Practice

Interactive Practice – Modal Verbs in Workplace Ethics

Instructions: Complete the activities below to practice using modal verbs (must, mustn’t, have to, don’t have to, should, ought to, can, could, might) in the context of workplace ethics. Use the dropdowns or text inputs to answer, then click "Check Answers" for each activity to see feedback.

🧠 Exercise 1 – Multiple Choice: Choose the Correct Modal

Instructions: Select the modal verb that best fits the sentence based on the context of workplace ethics. Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

  • 1. Leaders _____ create a safe space where employees feel comfortable discussing ethical issues.
  • 2. You _____ ignore reports of harassment, as it damages the company’s culture.
  • 3. Employees _____ attend optional ethics workshops, but they’re highly recommended.
  • 4. We _____ organize team discussions to address workplace favoritism.

💡 Tip: Choose the modal that matches the speaker’s stance—e.g., strong necessity (must), prohibition (mustn’t), or possibility (could).

🧠 Exercise 2 – Sentence Completion

Instructions: Type the correct modal verb to complete each sentence about workplace ethics. Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

  • 1. Companies _____ talk about values every day to foster an ethical culture.
  • 2. You _____ make decisions based on favoritism, as it undermines fairness.
  • 3. Mentors _____ help younger workers reflect on their ethical choices.
  • 4. Ignoring small ethical issues _____ lead to bigger problems in the company.

💡 Tip: Use modals that reflect the ethical urgency or possibility described—e.g., recommendation (should), prohibition (mustn’t), or uncertainty (might).

🧠 Exercise 3 – Apply Modals to Workplace Scenarios

Instructions: Read each workplace scenario and select the sentence with the correct modal verb to address the situation ethically. Click "Check Answers" for feedback.

  • 1. An employee notices gossip spreading in the team, which could harm morale.
  • 2. A manager sees an employee being treated unfairly by a colleague.
  • 3. A new intern is unsure how to handle a client’s unethical request.
  • 4. A team is considering whether to report a minor ethical lapse.

💡 Tip: Match the modal to the scenario’s ethical urgency—e.g., strong duty (must), suggestion (should), or possibility (could).
